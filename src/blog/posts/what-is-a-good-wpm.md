# What is a Good Typing Speed? WPM Guide for 2026

What is a good typing speed? For most adults, typing at **40 words per minute (WPM) is considered average**, while **60 WPM is above average**, and **80+ WPM is professional level**. In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down typing speed benchmarks by age, profession, and skill level so you can understand exactly where you stand.

## Understanding WPM: The Standard Measurement

WPM stands for "Words Per Minute" — the universal standard for measuring typing speed. One "word" is standardized as five characters (including spaces), which represents the average length of an English word. This standardization allows for consistent measurement across different typing tests, including [Typex's free typing test](/).

### How WPM is Calculated

Your WPM score is calculated using this formula:

```
WPM = (Number of Characters Typed ÷ 5) ÷ Time in Minutes
```

For example, if you type 300 characters in 60 seconds:
- 300 ÷ 5 = 60 words
- 60 ÷ 1 minute = **60 WPM**

**Accuracy matters too.** A raw WPM count doesn't account for errors. Professional tests use "net WPM" which subtracts errors from your gross WPM to give a true measure of your productive output.

## Typing Speed Requirements by Profession

Different careers have vastly different typing speed requirements. Here's what various professions typically need:

### Data Entry Professionals: 60-80 WPM Required

Data entry clerks must input large volumes of information quickly and accurately. Most positions require at least **60 WPM with 95%+ accuracy**. Some specialized medical or legal data entry roles demand **80+ WPM**.

### Administrative Assistants: 50-70 WPM

Administrative roles involve extensive email correspondence, report writing, and document preparation. **50 WPM is typically the minimum**, with **60+ WPM preferred** for competitive positions.

### Programmers and Developers: 40-60 WPM

While coding involves more thinking than typing, developers still type extensively. **40 WPM is sufficient** for most programming work since code quality matters more than input speed. However, faster typing helps during documentation and communication.

### Writers and Journalists: 60-80 WPM

Professional writers benefit enormously from faster typing speeds. When inspiration strikes, being able to type at **70+ WPM** allows thoughts to flow onto the page without interruption. Many successful writers maintain **80-100 WPM**.

### Medical Transcriptionists: 70+ WPM

Medical transcription requires keeping up with spoken dictation. The industry standard is **70 WPM minimum**, with **90+ WPM preferred** for high-volume work.

### Court Reporters: 225+ WPM (Using Stenography)

Using specialized stenography machines rather than standard keyboards, court reporters achieve speeds that seem superhuman. Their certification requires **225 WPM** with 95% accuracy.

### Gamers and Casual Users: 30-50 WPM

For casual computer use, gaming, and social media, **30-50 WPM** is perfectly adequate. Speed becomes important only when gaming competitively or participating in chat-heavy environments.

## How to Improve Your WPM

Ready to increase your typing speed? Here's a proven approach:

### 1. Learn Touch Typing

Touch typing — typing without looking at the keyboard — is the foundation of speed. Most people who look at the keyboard plateau at 30-40 WPM. Touch typists regularly achieve 60-100+ WPM.

Check out our [touch typing tutorial](/blog/touch-typing-tutorial) for a step-by-step guide.

### 2. Practice Daily (Even 15 Minutes)

Consistency beats intensity. **15 minutes of daily practice** yields better results than 2 hours once per week. Use our free typing test daily to track progress.

### 3. Focus on Accuracy First

Speed comes from accuracy. Every error requires correction, slowing you down. Aim for **95% accuracy** before pushing for higher speeds.

### 4. Use All Ten Fingers

Many people type with just 2-4 fingers, severely limiting their potential. Proper ten-finger technique can double or triple your speed.

### 5. Maintain Proper Posture

- Sit up straight with feet flat on the floor
- Wrists straight and slightly elevated (don't rest on the desk)
- Screen at eye level to prevent neck strain
- Take breaks every 20-30 minutes
